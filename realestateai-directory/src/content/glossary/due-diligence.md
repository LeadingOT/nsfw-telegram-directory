# What is Due Diligence

**Due diligence is the comprehensive investigation and analysis a buyer or investor conducts to verify the facts and assess the risks of a real estate transaction before finalizing the purchase.**

In real estate, due diligence is a critical period (typically 30-90 days after an accepted offer) where the buyer moves beyond surface-level appeal to perform an in-depth examination of the property. This process transforms the transaction from a conditional agreement into a firm, informed investment. The goal is to uncover any material issues—physical, financial, legal, or environmental—that could affect the property's value, usability, or the buyer's liability. It is the buyer's responsibility to perform this "homework," and the findings can be used to renegotiate the price, request repairs, or, in cases of severe undisclosed problems, legally withdraw from the contract without penalty.

The investigation covers multiple key areas. Physical due diligence involves professional inspections of the structure, roof, HVAC, plumbing, and electrical systems, as well as for pests like termites. Financial due diligence entails reviewing leases, operating statements, tax records, and existing utility bills. Legal due diligence examines the title report for liens or easements, verifies zoning compliance, and ensures all permits for renovations are in order. For larger or commercial properties, this phase may also include environmental site assessments to check for soil or groundwater contamination.

Thorough due diligence matters because it is the primary mechanism for risk mitigation in real estate. It prevents costly surprises after closing, such as an unexpected $20,000 roof replacement, the discovery of an unbuildable lot due to wetlands, or a tenant with a below-market lease that cannot be altered. For an investor, it validates the property's income potential. For a homeowner, it ensures the safety and soundness of their future home. Skipping or rushing this process can turn an investment into a financial burden.
