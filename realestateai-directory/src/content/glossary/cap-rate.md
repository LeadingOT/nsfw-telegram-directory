# What is Cap Rate

**Cap rate, or capitalization rate, is a fundamental financial metric used in real estate to estimate the potential return on an investment property, calculated by dividing the property's net operating income (NOI) by its current market value or purchase price.**

The cap rate formula is expressed as: **Cap Rate = Net Operating Income / Current Market Value**. Net Operating Income (NOI) is the property's annual income generated from rents and other sources, minus all necessary operating expenses (like property management, taxes, insurance, and maintenance), but crucially, before mortgage payments and income taxes. The result is expressed as a percentage. For example, a property generating $100,000 in NOI that is purchased for $1,250,000 has a cap rate of 8% ($100,000 / $1,250,000 = 0.08).

This percentage serves as a snapshot of a property's yield in a single year, assuming it was purchased entirely with cash. It is primarily a tool for comparison and risk assessment, not a comprehensive measure of total return. A higher cap rate generally implies a higher potential return but also suggests higher perceived risk (due to factors like location, property condition, or tenant quality). Conversely, a lower cap rate often indicates a lower-risk, more stable asset, frequently seen in prime markets.

Cap rates are critical for investors and analysts for several key use cases. They enable the quick comparison of different investment opportunities across markets and property types. Investors also use prevailing market cap rates to estimate a property's value based on its income (Value = NOI / Cap Rate), a process called capitalization. Furthermore, tracking cap rate trends in a market can signal whether valuations are rising or falling. For tools listed on RealEstateAI.tools, cap rate calculations are a core function, helping users automate income property analysis, screen deals, and generate investment reports efficiently.

**Related Terms:** Net Operating Income (NOI), Cash-on-Cash Return, Internal Rate of Return (IRR), Gross Rent Multiplier (GRM)
